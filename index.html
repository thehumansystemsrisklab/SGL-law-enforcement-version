Section 5 — Built-In Adoption Design
Why this won't become
shelf-ware
Every governance framework law enforcement has implemented through training has a consistent failure rate. POST training didn't fix Brady violations. CALEA accreditation didn't fix evidence room scandals. Body camera policy didn't fix footage suppression. The pattern is the same every time: when the governance requirement is separable from the workflow, people separate it from the workflow under pressure. The SGL is designed so that separation is structurally difficult — not through enforcement, but through design.
01
Workflow Gate Design
Condition as prerequisite, not parallel documentation
Key SGL conditions are designed as required fields in workflows the agency is already running — not separate documentation. An evidence item that cannot be logged without an EC1 custodian field populated means EC1 gets documented because the alternative is the item doesn't log. A case that cannot advance to active status without an I5 threshold entry means the threshold gets set because the alternative is the case doesn't open.
Vendor integration matters most here. RMS and evidence management vendors who build SGL condition fields as required workflow steps — rather than optional documentation — solve the adoption problem at the design level. The vendor data sharing framework creates the incentive for them to do it.
02
Live Score Visibility
Feedback in the moment, not at the audit
Governance frameworks fail because the person who skips the documentation and the person who bears the consequence are separated by years and often by role. The SGL produces a live GPWS score at the case level. A detective who can see their active case sitting at 0.58 because EC4 is absent has an actionable signal right now — not a compliance finding after the prosecution collapses. The score is visible in the normal case workflow, not in a separate audit interface.
The score display is a product design decision. It should live where the detective already works — not in a governance dashboard they only open when something goes wrong.
03
Friction-Reversed UX
Honest answer is the fastest answer
Most governance documentation fails because recording the inflated answer is faster than recording the honest one. If marking EC4 Present takes two clicks and marking EC4 Absent takes twelve fields, people mark Present. The SGL interface is designed so the most accurate answer is the least friction. Condition absent with gap noted takes fewer steps than condition present with full documentation. Partial (0.5) is faster to record than Present (1.0). The system rewards honesty with reduced friction.
The UX hierarchy — absent fastest, partial next, present requires most documentation — is a build specification commitment established before the interface is built.
04
Record–Evaluation Separation
The record belongs to the case, not the detective
A known governance inflation driver across documented case failures: when governance records are tied to personnel evaluation, the incentive is to document conditions as present regardless of reality. When a detective knows their GPWS scores appear in their annual review, the incentive is to document conditions as present regardless of reality. The SGL record is structurally separated from individual performance metrics. Absent conditions trigger structural remediation — what do we need to establish this condition — not disciplinary review. The record shows condition states, not personnel failures.
This is a deployment agreement term, not a software feature. HSRL specifies as part of agency deployment that the SGL record is not used in individual performance review. The blame and shame removal the SGL architecture is built around requires this to be explicit, not assumed.
What this is not
A training program
Training-based governance adoption has a consistent failure rate in law enforcement. None of the four mechanisms above are training solutions. All four are structural.
A compliance audit
Periodic audits find failures after they've produced consequences. The SGL surfaces governance gaps at the decision point — before the evidence is collected, before the charge is made, before the trial.
A personnel accountability tool
Governance records tied to individual performance create the incentive to inflate them. The SGL record is a case-level structural document. It tells you what conditions were present — not who is responsible for the ones that weren't.
How deployment works

Step 1 — Base SGL. Names an owner and sets the charge standard before the investigation runs. This alone moves best-outcome rate from 12% to 58%. It is the fastest, most immediate intervention and the right starting point for any agency.

Step 2 — Sub-SGLs. Each sub-SGL added beneath the base locks in the conditions that the base SGL cannot enforce on its own — the suppression check, the evidence chain, the witness record, the forensic standard. Best-outcome rate moves from 58% to 97%. The sub-SGLs are what close the remaining gap.

The financial case is in that gap. +211 additional cases cleared per year. $11.1M additional annual risk reduction. Both numbers represent what exists between a base SGL deployment and a fully active system. The sub-SGLs are not add-ons — they are what makes the base SGL worth the investment.

Section 4 — Governance Data & Vendor Improvement
What agencies can share
and why it matters
The SGL system generates structured governance data at the case level that has never existed in this form before. Agencies control what they share, with whom, and on what terms. Selective, anonymized sharing of that data creates a feedback loop that current law enforcement technology vendors have no access to — and cannot build tools to solve problems they cannot see.
What vendors have today
Activity data — logins, searches, report volume, case counts. Tells vendors how tools are used, not whether they produced good outcomes.
Clearance rate aggregates — jurisdictional totals from UCR. No case-level governance context. Cannot be linked to tool performance.
Anecdotal feedback — what detectives say in focus groups. Unstructured. Cannot drive systematic product improvement.
No outcome linkage — vendors cannot connect their tool's output to case resolution, conviction integrity, or wrongful conviction risk.
What the SGL system produces
Condition-level governance scores at T1, T2, T3 — per case, per condition, with outcome classification. For the first time: structured data linking governance state to case outcome.
Failure signature records — which structural signatures activated, at what point, in which case types. Tells vendors exactly where their tools interact with governance failures.
Remediation pathways — what governance interventions produced T1→T3 improvement, and which tools supported or failed to support them.
Cross-agency patterns — anonymized, aggregated condition absence rates by case type, era, and jurisdiction class.

What vendors can build with this data that they cannot build today
Digital Forensics Tools
Methodology disclosure built in
EC2 absence is the most common failure mode for digital evidence. A Cellebrite or MSAB extraction that auto-generates a methodology disclosure record — protocol name, version, extraction conditions — closes EC2 at the point of collection. Vendors who build this in become the governance-compliant option.
Evidence Management Systems
Chain custody auto-documentation
EC1 and ER3 failures — chain dissolution and access monitoring gaps — are both problems that evidence management software is positioned to solve structurally. Systems that produce an EC1/ER3-compliant record automatically at every transfer event eliminate the most operationally remediable governance failure in the dataset.
RMS / Case Management
Threshold prompt at case open
I5 — the charge threshold — is absent in 78% of worst-outcome cold cases. An RMS that prompts for a documented prosecutorial threshold before a case is formally opened, and flags when that field is blank at 30/60/90 days, solves the single highest-impact governance gap without changing how detectives work.
The market signal this creates
Law enforcement technology vendors currently compete on features. Once agencies are running the SGL and generating governance outcome data, they can compare tools on a dimension that has never existed before: does this tool help or hurt our governance scores? A digital forensics tool that closes EC2 at collection will produce measurably better governance outcomes than one that doesn't. An evidence management system that auto-documents EC1 at every transfer will show up in the data. Vendors that build to the governance standard gain a defensible, data-backed differentiator. Vendors that don't will eventually be visible in agency outcome records as a governance liability rather than an asset.

The agency controls all of this. The data stays with them. What they choose to share — and the terms on which they share it — is their leverage in every vendor conversation they have after deployment.
